A Unitary Transform of an i.i.d. Normal Random Variable

Consider an independent identically distributed Normal random variable x\mathbf{x}x with mean μ∈CC\mu \in \mathbb{C}^Cμ∈CC and covariance Σ∈CC×C\Sigma \in \mathbb{C}^{C \times C}Σ∈CC×C. Let x\mathbf{x}x have NNN vector-valued pixels, each with CCC channels, i.e. x∈RNC\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{NC}x∈RNC and

x[i]∼N(μ, Σ)∀1≤i≤N. \mathbf{x}[i] \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, ~ \Sigma) \quad \forall \quad 1 \leq i \leq N. x[i]∼N(μ, Σ)∀1≤i≤N.

Further, let x\mathbf{x}x have the channel-wise vectorization x=[x1T, x2T, …, xCT]T\mathbf{x} = [\mathbf{x}_1^T, \, \mathbf{x}_2^T, \, \dots, \, \mathbf{x}_C^T]^Tx=[x1T​,x2T​,…,xCT​]T. We can then write this via Kronecker product notation as

x∼N(μ⊗1N, Σ⊗IN). \mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu \otimes \mathbf{1}_N, ~ \Sigma \otimes I_N). x∼N(μ⊗1N​, Σ⊗IN​).

Now, consider a linear transformation y=Ax\mathbf{y} = A\mathbf{x}y=Ax. We know from linearity of expectation that E[Ax]=AE[x]=A(μ⊗1N)\mathbb{E}[A\mathbf{x}] = A\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}] = A(\mu \otimes \mathbf{1}_N)E[Ax]=AE[x]=A(μ⊗1N​) and that Cov(Ax)=ACov(x)AH\mathrm{Cov}(A\mathbf{x}) = A\mathrm{Cov}(\mathbf{x})A^HCov(Ax)=ACov(x)AH.

Further, consider a channel-wise unitary matrix, i.e. A=(IC⊗Q)A = (I_C \otimes Q)A=(IC​⊗Q) where Q∈CN×NQ \in \mathbb{C}^{N\times N}Q∈CN×N is unitary. The Kronecker notation here means that AAA is a block diagonal matrix with CCC blocks of QQQ on its diagonal. So, the mean and covariance of our transformed variable are

E[Ax]=(IC⊗Q)(μ⊗1N)Cov(Ax)=(IC⊗Q)(Σ⊗IN)(IC⊗Q)H \mathbb{E}[A\mathbf{x}] = (I_C \otimes Q)(\mu \otimes \mathbf{1}_N) \qquad \mathrm{Cov}(A\mathbf{x}) = (I_C \otimes Q)(\Sigma \otimes I_N)(I_C \otimes Q)^H E[Ax]=(IC​⊗Q)(μ⊗1N​)Cov(Ax)=(IC​⊗Q)(Σ⊗IN​)(IC​⊗Q)H

The mixed-product property of Kronecker product tells us that, for sensible matrix sizes, (A⊗B)(C⊗D)=(AC⊗BD)(A \otimes B)(C \otimes D) = (AC \otimes BD)(A⊗B)(C⊗D)=(AC⊗BD). Thus,

E[Ax]=(μ⊗Q1N)Cov(Ax)=(Σ⊗IN), \mathbb{E}[A\mathbf{x}] = (\mu \otimes Q\mathbf{1}_N) \qquad \mathrm{Cov}(A\mathbf{x}) = (\Sigma \otimes I_N), E[Ax]=(μ⊗Q1N​)Cov(Ax)=(Σ⊗IN​),

as QQH=INQQ^H = I_NQQH=IN​. For zero-mean Normally distributed noise, this results in the channel-wise unitary transform of said noise having the exact same distribution as before the transformation.

Parallel MRI

This is a particularly useful result in MRI, where the measurement domain is a CCC-channel (coil) Fourier domain contaminated by zero-mean additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN), i.e. we model measurements (y∈CNC\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{C}^{NC}y∈CNC) of image (x∈CN\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{C}^Nx∈CN) with coil sensitivity operator S∈CNC×NS \in \mathbb{C}^{NC \times N}S∈CNC×N via

y=(IC⊗FN)Sx+v,v∼N(0, (Σ⊗IN)). \mathbf{y} = (I_C \otimes F_N)S\mathbf{x} + v, \quad v \sim \mathcal{N}(0, ~ (\Sigma \otimes I_N)). y=(IC​⊗FN​)Sx+v,v∼N(0, (Σ⊗IN​)).

where FNF_NFN​ is the N-dimensional DFT matrix. Equivalently we can write y∼N((IC⊗FN)Sx, (Σ⊗IN))\mathbf{y} \sim \mathcal{N}((I_C \otimes F_N)S\mathbf{x}, ~ (\Sigma \otimes I_N))y∼N((IC​⊗FN​)Sx, (Σ⊗IN​)). As the DFT matrix is unitary, we know from the above discussion that the (multi-coil) image domain signal is contaminated with noise from the same distribution,

(IC⊗FNH)y∼N(Sx, (Σ⊗IN)). (I_C \otimes F_N^H)\mathbf{y} \sim \mathcal{N}(S\mathbf{x}, ~ (\Sigma \otimes I_N)). (IC​⊗FNH​)y∼N(Sx, (Σ⊗IN​)).
